Decision Point F: Is the client living with the abuser?

Importance: Living with the abuser increases the risk and complexity of safety planning.

Safe Emergency Housing Options:

Domestic Violence Shelters: Confidential locations offering secure accommodation.

Confusing Aspect: Availability may be limited; shelters might have specific admission criteria.
Emergency Hotel Vouchers: Short-term accommodation funded by local agencies when shelters are full.

Confusing Aspect: New social workers may not know how to access these vouchers or which agencies provide them.
Decision Point L: Does the abuser have a history of stalking or escalating behavior?

Assessing Risk Level:

Stalking and Escalation: Indicate a higher risk of severe harm.

Confusing Aspect: Understanding how to assess risk levels using tools like the Danger Assessment.
Action: Prioritize safety planning, possibly involving law enforcement.

Risk: Involving authorities may escalate the situation; client consent is crucial unless mandatory reporting applies.
Decision Point O: Are there children involved?

Children's Involvement:

Witnessing Abuse: Can have long-term psychological effects.

Child Protective Services (CPS):

Confusing Aspect: Understanding when mandatory reporting to CPS is required.

Mandatory Reporting: If children are at risk of harm, social workers are often legally required to report.

Action: Balance the need to report with maintaining trust; inform the client about obligations.
Decision Point V: Does the client need legal assistance (e.g., restraining order, custody)?

Family Law Considerations:

Custody and Visitation: Domestic violence can impact custody arrangements.

Confusing Aspect: Navigating family court processes and understanding how domestic violence influences legal decisions.
Legal Aid Resources:

Specialized Legal Services: Some organizations focus on assisting survivors with family law matters.

Action: Refer the client to appropriate legal assistance.
Decision Point AJ: Does the abuser have access to the client's workplace or online activities?

Workplace Safety Planning:

Abuser's Access to Workplace: Increases risk to the client and possibly coworkers.

Action: Develop a plan that may include notifying security, changing work hours, or relocating the client within the organization.
Online Safety:

Digital Privacy: Abusers may monitor emails, social media, or use tracking devices.

Confusing Aspect: New social workers may not be familiar with digital safety measures.

Action: Educate the client on securing online accounts, using safe devices, and avoiding sharing location information.


Considerations for Social Workers
Risk Assessment:

Use Structured Tools: Employ validated assessments like the Danger Assessment to evaluate the risk of lethality.

Continuous Monitoring: Reassess risk regularly, as situations can change rapidly.

Children's Safety and Well-being:

Trauma-Informed Approach for Children: Recognize signs of trauma in children and refer to specialized services.

Collaboration with Child Services: Work collaboratively with CPS when involved, advocating for the family's needs.

Legal Knowledge Specific to Domestic Violence:

Protective Orders: Understand the types (e.g., emergency, temporary, permanent) and processes to obtain them.

Victim's Rights Laws: Familiarize yourself with laws that protect survivors in your jurisdiction.

Cohabitation Dynamics:

Economic Dependence: Clients may rely financially on the abuser, complicating decisions to leave.

Action: Explore financial resources, such as emergency funds, benefits, or employment opportunities.
Housing Barriers: Survivors may face challenges in finding housing due to credit issues, rental history, or discrimination.

Action: Connect with housing programs that prioritize survivors of domestic violence.
Stalking and Technology Safety:

Tech-Facilitated Abuse: Be aware of how technology can be used to harass or monitor.

Action: Provide resources on digital safety, such as changing passwords, checking for spyware, and adjusting privacy settings.
Cultural and Community Factors:

Cultural Sensitivity: Understand how cultural background may influence the client's experience and choices.

Community Resources: Identify culturally specific services that may be more acceptable or accessible to the client.

Client Autonomy and Empowerment:

Respecting Decisions: Clients may choose options that seem unsafe; it's important to support them while providing information.

Empowerment Strategies: Focus on building the client's confidence and ability to make informed decisions.

Interagency Collaboration:

Networking: Build relationships with domestic violence agencies, legal services, and law enforcement.

Coordinated Response: Participate in multidisciplinary teams when appropriate to provide comprehensive support.

Professional Boundaries and Safety:

Personal Safety: Be mindful of your own safety when interacting with potentially dangerous situations.

Boundaries: Maintain professional boundaries to avoid over-involvement or potential manipulation by the abuser.

Documentation and Confidentiality:

Secure Record-Keeping: Protect client information rigorously, especially when the abuser may attempt to access records.

Informed Consent: Clearly explain how information will be used and any limits to confidentiality.

Self-Care for Social Workers:

Emotional Impact: Recognize the emotional toll of working with domestic violence cases.

Support Systems: Utilize supervision, peer support, and self-care practices to maintain well-being.
