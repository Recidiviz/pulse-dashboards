By working with a counselor to enhance your ability to think through situations calmly and evaluate potential outcomes, you'll have more control over your choices. Improving your decision-making is a gradual process, but by starting with these small steps, you’ll begin to notice positive changes over time. # Structured Recreation

Structured recreation is about finding ways to spend your free time doing things that matter to you and give you a sense of purpose. Right now, a lot of your time revolves around high-risk activities tied to your environment, which can lead to more stress or trouble. By trying out structured activities that line up with your interests—like working with cars or learning practical skills—you can start building routines that are healthier and more productive. Over time, this might even open up paths to jobs or hobbies you’ll enjoy. Let’s take a look at some steps to ease into this process and make it work for you.

## Short-Term Structured Recreation (1-3 weeks)

1. Explore Vocational or Skill-Based Opportunities (Weeks 1-2)
   - Reach out to [Workforce Development Center](#e4eaaaf2-d142-11e1-b3e4-080027620cf9) about training programs tied to mechanics or hands-on work.
   - Contact [Career Pathways Employment](#e4eaaaf2-d142-11e1-b3e4-080027620cf8) to learn if they offer workshops or programs related to careers you're curious about.
   - **Goal:** By the end of week 2, gather clear information about any step-in programs or opportunities that interest you and feel realistic right now.

2. Test Out Light Activities That Fit Your Interests (Week 3)
   - Take one hour to explore your interest in cars, such as by visiting a community garage, tinkering with minor car fixes, or watching repair tutorials online.
   - Find one way to turn downtime into time directed at something productive and stress-relieving.
   - **Goal:** By week 3, try one hands-on or hobby-based activity to see what feels like a good fit for your routine.

## Long-Term Structured Recreation (2-3 months)

1. Commit to a Weekly Routine of Interest-Based Activities (Months 2-3)
   - If a vocational activity or program like [Workforce Development Center](#e4eaaaf2-d142-11e1-b3e4-080027620cf9) aligns with your interests, commit to showing up or engaging weekly. Start small with manageable tasks or short-term programs.
   - If formal programs still don’t feel like the right match yet, deepen your personal exploration of hands-on hobbies like mechanics or other interests you may not have had time to pursue.

   - **Goal:** By month 4, evaluate whether these activities are bringing value to your life and decide how you’d want to grow them further.

By starting small and focusing on activities that connect to your interests, you can build toward something bigger when the time is right. It's not about changing everything at once—these steps give you choices to try what feels good and useful now, and we can adjust from there.
