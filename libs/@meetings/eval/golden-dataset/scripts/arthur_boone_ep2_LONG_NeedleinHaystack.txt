Client: Yeah, but it’s about the ratio and the patience. You brown your sausage first. Get it nice and dark. You want all those little crispy bits stuck to the bottom of the pan. That’s the fond. That’s where the flavor lives.

PO: Do you take the sausage out of the pan before you add the flour?

Client: Depends on how much grease the sausage gave up. If it's a lean sausage, I might add a little butter. But usually, I just leave the meat right in there, sprinkle the flour over the top, and stir it up. You gotta let the flour cook for a couple of minutes, though.

PO: To get rid of the raw flour taste?

Client: Exactly. Nothing ruins a gravy faster than raw flour. It tastes like paste. You cook it till it smells a little nutty, then you pour in your milk. Whole milk, preferably. None of that skim milk nonsense.

PO: Oh, definitely. Skim milk is just water lying about being milk.

Client: [Laughs] That's exactly what it is. You pour the milk in slow, stirring the whole time so it doesn't clump. Bring it to a simmer, let it thicken, and hit it with a heavy dose of black pepper. 

PO: Lots of black pepper. That’s the secret, right?

Client: Has to be. Sausage gravy should have little black specks all through it. If it ain't got pepper, it's just white sauce. 

PO: You’re making me hungry. But breakfast is one thing, what about barbecue? I feel like everybody has strong opinions on barbecue.

Client: Barbecue ain't an opinion, it's a religion. And there’s a lot of false prophets out there. 

PO: [Laughs] Let me guess, you're a purist? 

Client: I just believe in doing it right. Wood, fire, smoke, and time. None of these pellet grills where you press a button and walk away. That ain't smoking meat. That's baking outdoors.

PO: You don't like the pellet grills? They're getting really popular. 

Client: They’re convenient, I’ll give 'em that. But you don't get the same flavor. A real offset smoker, where you have to tend the firebox, manage the airflow, feed it splits of real wood... that takes skill. You're actually cooking the meat, not just programming a computer to do it.

PO: What kind of wood do you prefer? 

Client: Depends on the meat. If I’m doing pork—like a Boston butt for pulled pork, or some spare ribs—I like fruit wood. Apple or cherry. It gives it a nice sweet smoke, turns the meat a beautiful mahogany color. 

PO: And for beef? Like brisket?

Client: Brisket needs something stronger to stand up to the beef flavor. Hickory is good, but oak is the gold standard. Post oak, if you can get it. That's what they use down in Texas. Mesquite is alright, but you gotta be careful with it. 

PO: Does mesquite get too bitter?

Client: Very quickly. It burns hot and fast, and the smoke is real pungent. If you over-smoke a brisket with mesquite, it’ll taste like an ashtray. You just want a kiss of smoke, not a mouthful of soot.

PO: I’ve never tried to smoke a brisket. It seems intimidating. I hear about guys staying up all night checking the temperature.

Client: It is an exercise in patience. A big packer brisket can take 14, 16 hours. You have to trim it right, leaving just a quarter-inch of the fat cap so it bastes the meat as it renders. Then you hit it with the rub. 

PO: What goes in your rub? Are you a secret recipe guy?

Client: I don't keep secrets, but I keep it simple. For beef, all you really need is coarse black pepper and kosher salt. Fifty-fifty mix. Maybe a little garlic powder if you're feeling fancy. But the beef and the smoke should be the stars of the show. 

PO: No sugar? No paprika?

Client: Not on a brisket. Sugar burns at those long cooking times. For pork ribs, sure, you put some brown sugar and paprika in the rub. Pork likes sweet. Beef likes savory. 

PO: And what about the "stall"? I’ve watched those barbecue shows where they talk about the meat stalling and panicking.

Client: The stall is real. Usually hits around 160, 165 degrees internal temperature. You'll be watching your thermometer climb steady for hours, and then it just stops. Sometimes it even drops a degree or two. 

PO: Why does it do that? 

Client: Evaporative cooling. The meat starts sweating. The moisture comes to the surface and evaporates, which cools the meat down, fighting the heat of the smoker. It can sit at 160 for three or four hours. It drives beginners crazy.

PO: So what do you do? Just wait it out?

Client: You can wait it out if you have the time. That gives you the best bark—that dark, crusty outside. But if you’re trying to get dinner on the table, you wrap it. 

PO: In aluminum foil?

Client: Foil works, they call that the Texas crutch. It traps the steam and pushes the meat right through the stall. But it softens your bark. I prefer unwaxed butcher paper. It breathes a little bit, lets some of the steam out, so you keep your crust, but it still speeds up the cooking.

PO: That makes sense. And you pull it off at what, 200 degrees?

Client: Around 203 is usually the magic number. But you don't go by temperature alone. You go by feel. You take your thermometer probe and slide it into the thickest part of the brisket. If it slides in with zero resistance, like poking room-temperature butter, it’s done. 

PO: Man, I can almost smell it. Do you let it rest? I know you're supposed to rest steaks.

Client: You absolutely have to rest it. If you pull a brisket off the smoker and slice right into it, all the juices will run out all over your cutting board, and you’ll be left with dry meat. You wrap it in an old towel and put it in an empty cooler for at least two hours. Four is better. 

PO: In a cooler? Like, with ice?

Client: No, no ice. An empty, dry cooler. It acts as an insulated hot box. The meat will hold its temperature safely in there for a long time, and the muscles relax and reabsorb all those juices. When you finally slice it, it should just pull apart perfectly.

PO: Okay, I’m officially starving now. What about sides? You can’t just eat a pound of brisket. Well, I mean, you could, but...

Client: You need sides to cut the richness of the meat. A good, sharp potato salad. Not that mushy stuff from the deli counter that tastes like pure mayonnaise. 

PO: How do you make yours? Mustard base?

Client: Definitely mustard. Yellow mustard, a little bit of mayo to bind it, celery for crunch, and you gotta have chopped pickles. Sweet relish if you like it sweet, but I prefer diced dill pickles. And hard-boiled eggs. 

PO: Do you put paprika on top?

Client: Just a dusting for color. But the real trick to potato salad is dressing the potatoes while they're still warm. 

PO: Really? I always thought you had to chill them first.

Client: If you chill them first, they seal up and don't absorb the flavors. When you drain the hot potatoes, splash a little apple cider vinegar over them right away. The starch soaks up the vinegar, gives it a tangy bite from the inside out. Then you mix in your dressing. 

PO: That is a great tip. I’m writing that one down mentally. What about baked beans? 

Client: Baked beans are essential. But you don't just open a can and heat 'em up. You use the can as a base, sure, but you doctor it. Add some brown sugar, a splash of molasses, some dry mustard powder, and throw in some burnt ends from the brisket or some diced bacon. 

PO: Do you cook them on the stove or in the smoker?

Client: Put the pan right in the smoker underneath the pork shoulder. Let the pork fat drip right down into the beans. 

PO: Oh wow. That sounds incredible. 

Client: It is. But if we're talking sides, the ultimate test of anybody's kitchen is their macaroni and cheese. 

PO: Oh, man. People get very defensive about their mac and cheese. 

Client: Because most people make it wrong. 

PO: [Laughs] Let me guess. Stovetop is wrong?

Client: Stovetop is for Tuesday night when you're in a hurry. If we're talking a Sunday dinner or a barbecue, it has to be baked. And it cannot be dry. There is nothing worse than a block of dry macaroni that you have to cut with a knife.

PO: I’ve had that. It just tastes like starchy cheese paste. 

Client: Exactly. You have to make a Mornay sauce. That’s a fancy French term, but it just means a cheese sauce starting with a roux. Butter, flour, milk, and then you melt your cheese into it. 

PO: What kind of cheese do you use? Just cheddar?

Client: Cheddar is the base, yeah. Sharp cheddar for flavor. But you need cheeses that melt well to keep it creamy. A little Gruyere is fantastic, but it's expensive. Monterey Jack works great. Maybe a little smoked Gouda if you want that barbecue profile. 

PO: Do you buy the pre-shredded cheese in the bags?

Client: Never. Never ever. 

PO: Why not? Just quality?

Client: It's not just quality. The pre-shredded stuff is coated in potato starch and cellulose to keep it from clumping in the bag. If you try to melt that into a sauce, it gets grainy and breaks. You have to buy the block and shred it yourself. It takes five extra minutes, but it changes the whole dish. 

PO: I actually did not know that about the starch coating. That explains why my cheese sauces always end up kind of gritty. 

Client: That's exactly why. Grate it yourself. Fold it into the pasta, pour it into a buttered dish, and then you need a topping. Some folks use breadcrumbs. I don't care for it. I like to just put an extra layer of sharp cheddar on top so it gets a crusty, browned cheese lid.

PO: Yeah, those crispy corner pieces are the best part.

Client: Everybody fights over the corners. That’s how you know it’s good. 

PO: All this talk is making me think about Thanksgiving. That’s like the Super Bowl of cooking. Do you usually cook for the holidays?

Client: Oh, I used to cook the whole spread. Turkey, ham, four or five sides, pies. It's a production. You gotta start planning a week in advance. 

PO: How do you do your turkey? Roasting, or are you one of those guys who deep fries it?

Client: Deep frying makes a fantastic bird. Skin is crispy, meat is juicy. But it's too much of a hassle, and it's dangerous if you don't know what you're doing. I've seen too many videos of people dropping a frozen turkey into boiling peanut oil and setting their driveway on fire.

PO: Yeah, the oil displaces and boils over. Pretty crazy.

Client: Water and hot oil don't mix. The ice turns to steam instantly and blows the oil right out of the pot. No, I prefer to brine it and roast it. Or smoke it, if the weather's nice. 

PO: Wet brine or dry brine? 

Client: I switched to dry brining a few years ago and never went back. Wet brining works, but hauling a five-gallon bucket of raw turkey water in and out of the fridge is a mess. Plus, the skin absorbs water, so it's hard to get it crispy in the oven.

PO: So how does dry brining work? Just rubbing it with salt?

Client: Kosher salt, black pepper, maybe some sage and thyme. You rub it all over the bird, under the skin too, and just leave it uncovered in the fridge for two or three days. 

PO: Uncovered? Doesn't it dry out?

Client: The skin dries out. That's exactly what you want. It looks a little leathery when it goes in the oven, but the meat inside stays incredibly juicy because the salt breaks down the muscle proteins. And since the skin is dry, it crisps up like potato chips. 

PO: That sounds way easier than the bucket method. 

Client: It is. But the real debate at Thanksgiving ain't the turkey. It's the dressing. 

PO: You say dressing, not stuffing. 

Client: Stuffing goes inside the bird. Dressing is baked in a pan on the side. I don't stuff the bird. By the time the stuffing inside reaches a safe temperature to kill the salmonella, the breast meat is overcooked to dust. Bake it on the side.

PO: What kind of dressing do you make? Bread or cornbread?

Client: Cornbread. Southern style. But it can't be sweet cornbread. You use that sweet, cake-like cornbread for dressing, and the whole dish tastes like dessert. You gotta make a savory cornbread in a cast iron skillet. 

PO: Using the seasoned skillet we were talking about earlier.

Client: Exactly. Bring it full circle. You bake the cornbread a day or two ahead, let it go slightly stale. Crumble it up with some day-old white bread or biscuits for texture. Then you add your celery, your onions, lots of sage. 

PO: Sage is the flavor of Thanksgiving. 

Client: It really is. And you bind it with chicken stock or turkey broth. You want it wet, almost soupy before it goes in the oven, because it's going to absorb all that liquid as it bakes. 

PO: What about dessert? Sweet potato or pumpkin pie?

Client: Sweet potato. No contest. 

PO: Really? I feel like pumpkin is the classic.

Client: Pumpkin is what people eat because they think they're supposed to. A pumpkin has no flavor on its own. It's just a vessel for cinnamon and nutmeg. A sweet potato actually has its own rich, earthy flavor. 

PO: Do you use fresh sweet potatoes or the canned ones?

Client: Always fresh. You roast 'em in the oven until the skins are blackened and peeling away. The sugars caramelize inside, they get real sweet and soft. You peel 'em, mash 'em up with butter, brown sugar, cinnamon, a little evaporated milk, and maybe a splash of bourbon. 

PO: Bourbon in the pie?

Client: Just a splash. The alcohol cooks out, but it leaves this deep, oaky flavor that pairs perfectly with the sweet potato. Pour that into a homemade crust, bake it till it sets. Best dessert on the table. 

PO: You make your own crust, too? I always buy the frozen ones.

Client: Frozen ones are okay in a pinch, but a homemade crust is easy if you know the secret. 

PO: Let me guess. Ice cold butter again?

Client: You got it. Ice cold butter, maybe a little shortening for flakiness, and ice water. The trick with pie crust is you want to leave little visible chunks of butter in the dough. You don't mix it until it's uniform. It should look a little shaggy.

PO: It sounds like baking is a lot about leaving things alone. Not overmixing, not overworking.

Client: That's ninety percent of cooking in general. People want to fuss with things. They want to poke the steak, flip the burgers five times, stir the rice. You just gotta let the heat do its job. 

PO: The stirring the rice thing is real. If I stir it, it turns to glue.

Client: Because you're releasing the starch. If you're making risotto, you want to stir it constantly to get it creamy. But if you want fluffy white rice, you bring it to a boil, drop it to a simmer, put the lid on, and walk away. Don't look at it, don't touch it.

PO: What about chili? Since we're moving into colder weather, that's my go-to. But I never know what the "rules" are. 

Client: Oh, there are rules. Depending on where you live. If you're in Texas, the rule is no beans. 

PO: Yeah, I've heard that. They call it a "bowl of red." Just meat and chiles.

Client: Right. And it's fantastic. You use chuck roast cut into little cubes, not ground beef. You make a paste out of dried ancho and guajillo peppers, cook it down slow. It’s rich, almost like a beef stew. 

PO: But what's your personal preference? Beans or no beans?

Client: I don't live in Texas, so I put beans in mine. [Laughs] I like kidney beans and pinto beans. It stretches the meal, adds some texture. 

PO: Ground beef or the cubed chuck you were talking about?

Client: I actually like a mix. I'll use some ground chuck for the base, but I'll throw in some diced stew meat or even some leftover smoked brisket if I have it. Gives it different textures. 

PO: Do you have a secret ingredient for your chili? A lot of people say they use chocolate or coffee.

Client: Both, actually. 

PO: Really? Does it taste like mocha?

Client: No, you don't use enough for it to taste like chocolate. Just a tablespoon of unsweetened cocoa powder, and maybe a half cup of strong black coffee instead of some of the beef broth. It just adds a deep, earthy bitterness that balances the sweetness of the tomatoes and the heat of the peppers. 

PO: I’ll have to try that. I usually just dump in a packet of chili seasoning and a can of diced tomatoes.

Client: That’ll feed you, but if you want to elevate it, toast your own spices. Get some whole cumin seeds, toast 'em in a dry skillet until they smell fragrant, then grind 'em up. Fresh ground cumin makes a world of difference compared to the stuff that's been sitting in a jar on the shelf for two years.

PO: It makes sense. Spices lose their potency over time. 

Client: Very quickly. That's why I buy 'em whole when I can. Same with black pepper. Never buy the pre-ground dust. It tastes like sawdust. Get a pepper grinder. 

PO: You're really an encyclopedia on this stuff. Did you ever cook professionally, or just for family?

Client: Just for family. My mother was a great cook, my grandmother was even better. You stand around the kitchen long enough as a kid, you absorb it. Plus, I like to eat well. If you're gonna eat three times a day for your whole life, you might as well figure out how to make it taste good.

PO: That is a very solid philosophy. Have you ever tried your hand at Cajun cooking? Like gumbo or jambalaya?

Client: Oh, yeah. Gumbo is another one of those dishes that requires patience. It all starts with the roux. But a gumbo roux is completely different than a gravy roux. 

PO: How so? Because you cook it longer?

Client: Much longer. A gravy roux is blonde. You cook it for two minutes. A gumbo roux, you use equal parts oil—not butter, because butter will burn—and flour, and you stir it over medium-low heat for about forty-five minutes to an hour. 

PO: An hour of stirring? My arm would fall off.

Client: You can't stop stirring, either. If you turn your back on it for thirty seconds, it'll scorch on the bottom. And if a roux scorches, you have to throw it out and start over. There's no saving it. It'll make the whole pot taste like an ashtray.

PO: What color are you looking for? Like peanut butter?

Client: Darker. Peanut butter is good for a lighter seafood gumbo, maybe. But for a chicken and sausage gumbo, you want it the color of an old copper penny. Or dark chocolate. It smells like roasted nuts. At that stage, it doesn't thicken the soup as much, but the flavor is incredible. 

PO: And then you add the celery, onions, and bell peppers?

Client: The Holy Trinity. Yep. You dump those cold vegetables right into that boiling hot roux, and it sizzles and drops the temperature immediately, which stops the roux from cooking any further. That's when you know you're safe. 

PO: Do you put tomatoes in your gumbo?

Client: [Sighs] You’re asking all the controversial questions today. 

PO: [Laughs] I didn't know that was controversial!

Client: Oh, it's a huge divide in Louisiana. Creole style gumbo from New Orleans uses tomatoes. Cajun style from the country, the bayous, does not. I lean towards the Cajun style. No tomatoes. Just a rich, dark broth, chicken, Andouille sausage, and maybe some okra to thicken it. 

PO: Does okra make it slimy? That's always been my fear with okra.

Client: It can, if you don't treat it right. If you slice it and roast it in a hot oven first, or fry it up in a skillet before adding it to the pot, it cooks out that mucilage—that's the slimy stuff. Then it just acts as a thickener without the weird texture.

PO: Good to know. I’ve always avoided okra. What about seafood? Do you cook a lot of fish?

Client: I like a good fish fry. Catfish, usually. Soaked in buttermilk or hot sauce, then dredged in cornmeal. You want a fine cornmeal, not the gritty stuff, and you fry it at exactly 350 degrees. 

PO: So the oil temperature is crucial there, too.

Client: For frying anything, temperature is everything. If the oil is too cold, the fish just absorbs the grease and gets soggy. If it's too hot, the crust burns before the fish cooks through. If you keep it right at 350, the moisture in the fish turns to steam and actually pushes the oil away, giving you a crisp, dry crust.

PO: It sounds like cooking is just as much science as it is art. 

Client: It is. Once you understand the science of it—why the dough rises, why the meat stalls, how the oil reacts—the art part is just playing with flavors.

PO: What’s your favorite thing to just throw together when you don't feel like doing a big production? Like a quick weeknight dinner.

Client: Can't beat a good smash burger. 

PO: Oh, man, those are great. You smash them really thin on a griddle?

Client: Exactly. Cast iron skillet again. Get it ripping hot. Take a two-ounce ball of eighty-twenty ground beef, put it in the dry pan, and smash it flat with a heavy spatula. Hold it down for ten seconds so it sears to the metal. 

PO: Does it stick?

Client: You want it to stick at first. That's how you get that crust. You hit it with salt and pepper, let it cook for maybe sixty seconds. Then you use a sharp metal spatula to scrape it off, flip it, put a slice of American cheese on it, and it's done thirty seconds later. 

PO: Just American cheese?

Client: For a burger? Always American. It melts better than anything else. Put two of those thin patties on a toasted bun with some pickles and a little mayo. Takes five minutes from start to finish, and it beats anything you can get at a drive-thru.

PO: I might have to make that for dinner tonight. It sounds too good. Do you grow any of your own vegetables to go with this stuff?

PO: Why is that? The store ones always taste like water.

Client: Because they're picked green so they can survive shipping, and then they're gassed with ethylene to turn 'em red. They never ripened on the vine, so they never developed any sugars. A real homegrown tomato, a big Cherokee Purple or a Brandywine, it's heavy, it's juicy, it's sweet and acidic at the same time.

Client: Exactly. You've got your drag set right, or else they'll just snap your line like thread. You just gotta lean back, keep the tension, and let them tire themselves out. Sometimes it takes twenty, thirty minutes just to get 'em close to the boat.

Client: Both, but I preferred the boat. Had a little fourteen-foot jon boat. Flat bottom, painted olive green. Had a little trolling motor on the back, but half the time I just used oars so I wouldn't spook the fish in the shallows.

PO: There's something very peaceful about rowing. Just the sound of the water.

Client: It's a rhythm. Dip, pull, glide. Dip, pull, glide. And the water drops coming off the oars look like little glass beads in the early morning light. You get out there before the sun clears the tree line, and there's that mist just hovering right on the surface. 

PO: I know exactly what you mean. It looks almost like smoke.

Client: Yeah. And the water is like glass. Perfect mirror. You cast a lure out, a little topwater popper, and it lands with a tiny 'plip'. You let the ripples clear... give it a twitch.

PO: And then they strike?

Client: If you're lucky. And when a bass hits a topwater lure in the dead quiet of the morning, it sounds like somebody dropped a bowling ball in the lake. 'Ker-ploosh!' Scares you half to death if you ain't ready for it.

PO: [Laughs] I can imagine. I've mostly just done bobber fishing. You know, sitting in a lawn chair waiting for the little red and white ball to go under.

Client: Hey, ain't nothing wrong with bobber fishing. That's how we all learn. It teaches you patience. You learn to watch the water. Sometimes the bobber doesn't go all the way under. Sometimes it just... lays over on its side. Or it moves a couple inches to the left. 

PO: What does that mean when it lays on its side?

Client: Usually means a fish picked up the bait and swam up toward the surface with it, taking the weight off the line. If you ain't paying attention, you'll miss the strike entirely. 

PO: See, I probably missed dozens of fish not knowing that. I'd only reel in if the bobber completely disappeared.

Client: Oh, they're sneaky. Especially the older, bigger fish. They didn't get big by being stupid. They'll mouth a bait, taste it, and spit it out before you even know theyre there. That's why I always liked feeling the line with my finger.

PO: How does that work?

Client: You keep your index finger resting right on the fishing line just above the reel. Even if the wind is blowing and putting slack in the line, you can feel the vibration through your fingertip. It feels like a tiny little 'tick... tick'. Soon as you feel that, you drop the rod tip, reel up the slack, and set the hook hard.

PO: You make it sound like an art form.

Client: It's more of a conversation, I guess. You're trying to figure out what they want, and they're trying to figure out if it's safe. 

PO: Did you ever do any fly fishing? The kind where you're whipping the line back and forth?

Client: A little bit. Up in the mountain streams, mostly. It's a whole different ballgame. Beautiful to watch, but it'll wear your arm out. And the trees... man, fly fishing around trees is an exercise in frustration. 

PO: Catching a lot of branches?

Client: I've caught more oak trees than trout, I'll tell you that much. You get that backcast going, and you don't realize how far back the line travels. Next thing you know, your hundred-dollar hand-tied fly is forty feet up in a sycamore tree. 

PO: That's got to be annoying. Do you just break the line?

Client: Usually. Sometimes you tug on it hoping the branch bends, but then the fly comes rocketing back at your face at sixty miles an hour. You only make that mistake once. 

PO: Good point. A hook to the face doesn't sound like a good time.

Client: No sir. But when you get it right, and that fly lands perfectly softly on a riffle in the stream, and a rainbow trout comes up and just sips it off the surface... man. The colors on a wild rainbow trout. Pictures don't do 'em justice. They literally look like they've got a stripe of neon painted down their side.

PO: I've only ever seen them mounted on walls. 

Client: Yeah, but by then the color fades. You gotta see 'em fresh out of the cold water. They're slippery and vibrant. I always let the trout go. Never had the heart to keep 'em. 

PO: Really? I thought trout was supposed to be great.

Client: Oh, some folks love it. But a wild trout is too pretty. Catfish, though? I'll string up a catfish in a heartbeat. They're ugly anyway. Big ol' whiskers and beady little eyes. 

PO: [Laughs] So beauty saves you in the fish world.

Client: Pretty much. Speaking of things growing in the dirt, though... fishing always made me think of gardening. 

PO: Because of the worms?

Client: Partially. But just the patience of it. You ever keep a garden?

PO: I tried growing some cherry tomatoes on my balcony once. The squirrels got to them before they even turned red.

Client: Oh, squirrels are the devil's own farmhands, I swear. They won't even eat the whole tomato. They'll just take one bite out of it and leave it on the railing just to spite you.

PO: Yes! That's exactly what happened. Just one bite out of the side of every single one. I was furious.

Client: It's a war. Gardening is warfare, disguised as a peaceful hobby. You're fighting the weather, the bugs, the squirrels, the deer, the rabbits, and sometimes the soil itself.

PO: What do you mean by fighting the soil?

Client: Well, down where I grew up, we had clay. Thick, red clay. When it's wet, it sticks to your boots like concrete, and when it dries out in August, it cracks open and turns hard as a brick. Try getting a carrot root to grow straight down through that.

PO: So how did you manage it? 

Client: You gotta amend it. We spent years hauling in compost, sand, peat moss, old leaves... just working it into the dirt year after year until it turned into dark, crumbly loam. Good dirt smells sweet, you know? Like a cellar after a rain. 

PO: I don't think I've ever smelled truly good dirt. Just the potting soil from the hardware store.

Client: Potting soil is fine for a balcony, but real earth has life in it. You scoop up a handful of good garden soil, and you'll see earthworms, little beetles, all sorts of things moving around. That means the soil is breathing. 

PO: What all did you grow?

Client: Everything under the sun. Silver Queen sweet corn, Kentucky Wonder pole beans, crookneck squash, zucchini... and tomatoes. Lots and lots of tomatoes. 

PO: Were they the big ones or the cherry kind?

Client: We grew the big heirlooms. Brandywine, Cherokee Purple, Mortgage Lifters. You ever see a Cherokee Purple tomato?

PO: I don't think so. Are they actually purple?

Client: Sort of a bruised, dark pinkish-purple, with green shoulders up near the stem. They look ugly as sin to someone who's only ever seen those perfect, shiny red tomatoes at the supermarket. But the smell... you brush up against a tomato vine in the heat of the day, and that sharp, green, earthy smell hits you. Nothing else in the world smells like a tomato plant.

PO: That's true. It's a very distinct scent. A little peppery, almost.

Client: Exactly. Peppery. I used to go out early in the morning, while the dew was still on the leaves, and just walk the rows. Checking for hornworms. 

PO: What's a hornworm?

Client: Oh, you don't want to know. Huge, bright green caterpillars, thick as your thumb, with a little red horn on their tail end. They blend in perfectly with the leaves. You won't see 'em, but you'll notice half a branch has been stripped bare overnight. 

PO: How do you get rid of them? Do you have to spray the plants?

Client: Nah, we didn't use sprays. My granddad made us pick 'em off by hand. You just grab 'em and pluck 'em off. They stick to the stems pretty good, little feet like velcro. 

PO: Picking off giant caterpillars by hand sounds like a nightmare.

Client: It builds character! We'd toss 'em in a coffee can and take 'em down to the creek for fish bait. The bluegill loved 'em. It's the circle of life, right there in the backyard. 

PO: So you just recycled the pests into fish bait. That's pretty smart.

Client: You use what you got. Same with the corn stalks. Once the corn was done for the year, we'd cut the stalks down and let 'em dry out, use 'em for mulch around the base of the fruit trees. Everything had a second purpose.

PO: Did you have a lot of fruit trees too?

Client: Just a few. A couple of peach trees, an old gnarly apple tree, and a pecan tree that was probably a hundred years old. That pecan tree cast a shadow the size of a barn. 

PO: Did it actually produce pecans? 

Client: Oh, baskets of 'em. But getting to 'em before the crows and the squirrels was a race. And the crows are smart. They'd pick a pecan, fly up high over the paved driveway, and drop it to crack the shell.

PO: Wait, really? They figured out how to use the driveway as a nutcracker?

Client: Absolutely. I'd sit on the porch and watch 'em. Drop it, swoop down, see if it broke. If it didn't, they'd pick it up, fly a little higher, and drop it again. Smartest birds in the woods. 

PO: That's incredible. I've heard crows are highly intelligent, but seeing it in action would be wild.

Client: They've got a whole language, too. When I used to go deer hunting, the crows were the best alarm system in the forest. 

PO: How so?

Client: Well, if you're sitting quiet in a tree stand, and everything is still... if a coyote or a bobcat starts moving through the brush, the crows see it from above. They'll start making a real specific, sharp 'caw-caw-caw'. It's an alarm call. Different from their casual chatter. 

PO: So they give away the predators.

Client: Yep. And they'll do it for humans, too. If another hunter was walking through the woods half a mile away, the crows would track him, yelling the whole time. You could literally trace the path of whoever was walking by listening to the crows yelling overhead. 

PO: That must make hunting a lot easier if you know what to listen for.

Client: Or harder, if the crows are yelling at *you*. If you're trying to sneak to your spot and the crows sound the alarm, every deer in a one-mile radius knows exactly where you are. 

PO: I never thought about that. You're trying to be stealthy, but the whole forest is watching you.

Client: The woods are never as empty as they look. You sit in a stand long enough, the woods forget you're there. That's the secret to hunting, really. Just becoming part of the tree. 

PO: Sounds like a lot of waiting.

Client: Ninety-five percent of hunting is just waiting. Freezing your toes off, staring at the same oak sapling for four hours until your eyes start playing tricks on you. Every shadow looks like a buck. Every squirrel digging in the dry leaves sounds like a three-hundred-pound monster walking towards you. 

PO: I've heard squirrels are surprisingly loud in the woods.

Client: They sound like bulldozers! A little gray squirrel can make more noise kicking up dry oak leaves than a full-grown buck. A deer... a deer is a ghost. They can walk through a dry brush pile and not snap a single twig. It defies the laws of physics. 

PO: How do you even spot them, then, if they're that quiet?

Client: You don't look for the whole deer. If you're scanning the woods looking for a brown horse with antlers, you'll never see 'em. You look for a horizontal line in a vertical forest. The trees go up and down. A deer's back goes left to right. 

PO: That's a great tip. Horizontal lines. 

Client: Yeah. Or you look for a flicker of movement. The flick of an ear, or the white of their tail. Sometimes they just materialize. You look at a patch of brush, nothing's there. You blink, and suddenly there's a doe standing right there looking at you. 

PO: Did you use a rifle or a bow?

Client: Rifle, mostly. Old thirty-thirty lever action. Same kind John Wayne used in the movies. Not fancy, but it gets the job done in thick brush. Bow hunting takes a level of dedication I never quite had. You gotta get 'em in real close. 

PO: Plus the wind direction matters a lot more, right?

Client: Oh, wind is everything. If the wind is blowing from you toward the deer, you might as well pack up and go home. They'll smell you a quarter mile away. We used to tie a little piece of cotton thread to the barrel of the rifle, just to watch which way the faintest breeze was blowing. 

PO: The level of strategy is intense. It's not just walking into the woods.

Client: No, it's a chess match. Honestly, some of my favorite hunts were days I never even pulled the trigger. Just watching the sun come up, seeing a fox trot by, maybe watching a woodpecker tear up a dead pine tree. 

PO: Pileated woodpeckers? The really big ones?

Client: Yes sir. Big red crests on their heads. They look like pterodactyls flying through the woods. And when they hammer on a hollow tree, it echoes like a machine gun. Brrrrrrrrrrt. 

PO: I saw one of those in a state park once. I couldn't believe how big it was. 

Client: Big as a crow. They'll shred a dead tree looking for carpenter ants. Leaves wood chips scattered all over the base of the trunk. 

PO: It sounds like you spent a lot of time just observing nature.

Client: You know, it's the best show in town, and it's free. We'd pack up the old truck, drive a couple hours out into the middle of nowhere, and just disappear for the weekend. No roads, no noise, just the wind. 

PO: Did you take a lot of road trips when you were younger? 

Client: Quite a few. Mostly just wandering. Before everything was connected by interstates, we used to take the old two-lane blacktops. Route 66, Highway 81. You could drive for hours and just see fields, old rusted-out gas stations, little diners that looked like they hadn't changed since the fifties. 

PO: I love taking the scenic routes. The interstate is fast, but it's so boring. Every exit looks exactly the same. 

Client: The exact same fast food joints, the exact same gas stations. You don't even know what state you're in unless you see the welcome sign. But back in the day, you had to navigate. You had a big paper map folded up in the glovebox. 

PO: And trying to fold it back up exactly the way it came was impossible.

Client: Nearly impossible! You'd end up with a map that had eighty new creases in it, bulging out of the glove compartment. But you'd trace your route with your finger. 'Take 40 West until we hit this little county road, then go north at the water tower.' 

PO: Did you get lost often?

Client: All the time. But getting lost was part of the fun. You'd take a wrong turn, end up on some gravel road that climbed up a mesa, and suddenly you've got a view of a canyon that's not on any tourist brochure. We'd just pull over, sit on the tailgate, and watch the sun go down. 

PO: Did you camp in the truck or set up a tent?

Client: Usually just slept in the bed of the truck. Had an old camper shell on the back. Throw a couple of moving blankets down, maybe a sleeping bag if it was chilly. Keep the tailgate down if there wasn't any rain. You'd wake up to the sound of crickets and the smell of sagebrush. 

PO: That sounds amazing, honestly. Just completely unplugged.

Client: Out west, the night sky is different, too. Away from the city lights, the stars look like somebody spilled salt across a piece of black velvet. You can see the Milky Way clear as day. 

PO: I went out to West Texas once and couldn't believe the stars. It almost gives you vertigo looking up.

Client: It makes you feel awful small, doesn't it? Just a speck on a speck in the middle of all that empty space. It's humbling. I remember driving through the high desert in New Mexico in an old Ford F-150. Truck didn't have air conditioning, so we just kept the windows rolled down. The wind out there is dry, hot, feels like a blow dryer. 

PO: Driving through the desert with no AC sounds rough during the day.

Client: You learned to drive early morning and late evening. During the heat of the day, you find some shade, pop the hood to let the engine cool off, and wait it out. Cars used to overheat all the time on those long grades. You'd always carry a couple gallon jugs of water just for the radiator.

PO: Did you ever break down out there?

Client: Once, outside of a little town called Tucumcari. Blew a radiator hose. Steam hissing out from under the hood, smelled like sweet syrup from the antifreeze. We coasted to a stop on the shoulder. 

PO: What do you do in the middle of nowhere with a blown hose? 

Client: You get creative. I had a roll of duct tape and a pair of hose clamps in my toolbox. Wrapped that hose tight as I could, clamped it down, poured the drinking water into the radiator, and limped it ten miles into town at twenty miles an hour. 

PO: That's a true MacGyver fix.

Client: It held! The mechanic in town just laughed when he saw it. He charged me five bucks for a new hose and we were back on the road in an hour. People were more helpful back then, I think. You see someone on the side of the road with the hood up, you stopped. It was the unwritten rule of the highway.

PO: Yeah, nowadays people just zip past at eighty miles an hour. Everyone assumes you have a cell phone to call a tow truck anyway.

Client: Exactly. The cell phone killed the roadside rescue. But back then, if you didn't stop to help, that guy might be stranded out there all night. I met some interesting folks pulling over to help change a flat tire. 

PO: Any memorable ones?

Client: I helped this old couple once in Arkansas. They were driving a giant Cadillac land yacht. Things were bigger than a bass boat. They had a blowout on the rear passenger side, and the man was wearing a full seersucker suit in ninety-degree heat, trying to wrestle the jack out of the trunk.

PO: Oh man, a seersucker suit on the side of a dusty highway.

Client: He looked miserable. I told him to sit in the AC, and I changed the tire for 'em. Took me ten minutes. When I was done, his wife comes out and hands me a jar of homemade peach preserves. Said it was the only way she knew how to pay me. 

PO: Peach preserves are better than cash sometimes.

Client: I'd take a jar of good preserves over a twenty-dollar bill any day of the week. We opened it that night, sitting on the tailgate, just eating it right out of the jar. 

PO: You can't replicate that kind of road trip experience. It's the spontaneity of it.

Client: That's the word. Spontaneity. Now everything is planned out. You got your GPS telling you your exact arrival time down to the minute. You got apps telling you which gas station is three cents cheaper. We used to just drive until the needle hit 'E' and hope a station showed up over the next hill. 

PO: That sounds incredibly stressful to me. I start sweating when my gas light comes on.

Client: You get used to the gamble. Though I did run out of gas once in the middle of the Ozarks. Coasted down a hill and came to a dead stop right at the bottom of a valley. Had to walk three miles with a plastic jerry can to a bait shop that had one rusty gas pump out front. 

PO: Three miles in the Ozarks? That's not flat terrain. 

Client: Uphill both ways, I swear. And carrying a full can of gas back is heavy. You switch hands every hundred yards, gas sloshing against your leg. But you know what? The trees along that road were magnificent. Huge, towering oaks and hickories. The leaves were just starting to turn yellow.

PO: So you at least had a nice view on your walk.

Client: Beautiful view. And there was a little creek running right alongside the road. The sound of water tumbling over river rocks... it kept me company the whole walk back. I kept thinking, 'Man, if I had my fishing pole, I wouldn't even care about the truck.' 

PO: Always looking for the next fishing hole.

Client: Any body of water looks like a fishing hole to me. I look at drainage ditches and wonder if there's a stray bluegill in there. 

PO: [Laughs] I don't think you'd want to eat a drainage ditch fish.

Client: Lord, no. But just the tug on the line is enough. I used to practice casting in my backyard with a rubber weight tied to the end of the line. Just aim for coffee cans set up across the lawn. 

PO: To work on your accuracy?

Client: Yeah. When you're fishing in heavy cover, with lots of submerged logs and low-hanging branches, you've got to be able to drop a lure into a circle the size of a dinner plate from thirty feet away. If you miss, you're snagged on a stump and you lose your lure. 

PO: It sounds like pitching horseshoes.

Client: Very similar mechanics. It's all in the wrist. You don't whip the rod around like a baseball bat. You just flick it. Let the flex of the rod do the work. The fiberglass bends, loads up the energy, and then *snap*, it shoots the lure out right where you look. 

PO: I usually just end up hooking the bush behind me.

Client: [Laughs] That's the classic maneuver. The overhead cast. It's dangerous on a crowded boat. I've nearly lost an ear or two to buddies casting without looking behind them. 

PO: Yikes. Yeah, hooks flying around. I'd imagine eye protection is a good idea.

Client: Polarized sunglasses are the best tool a fisherman has. Not just to protect your eyes from hooks, but to see into the water. The polarization cuts the glare off the surface. You can literally see down into the shallows. You see the rocks, the drop-offs, and sometimes, you see the fish cruising. 

PO: Oh, so it gives you a massive advantage. You're not casting blind.

Client: Exactly. Sight fishing, they call it. If the water is clear enough, you can watch the fish swim up to your lure, stare at it, and turn away. It's incredibly frustrating. 

PO: To see them reject it in real-time. 

Client: It hurts your pride. You think you've got this irresistible presentation, and a three-pound bass just swims up, looks at it like it's a piece of trash, and slowly swims back under a log. Makes you want to throw your whole tackle box in the lake. 

PO: That's hilarious. They're critics.

Client: The harshest critics in nature. You've got to change the lure, maybe switch the color. If it's a cloudy day, you use darker colors. If it's bright out, you use bright, reflective colors. 

PO: Wait, shouldn't you use dark colors in the dark, and bright colors in the sun? Or vice versa? I'm confused.

Client: Actually, dark colors on a cloudy day create a stronger silhouette against the sky when the fish looks up. So a black or dark blue lure stands out better against gray clouds. 

PO: That makes sense. I never would have thought of the silhouette. 

Client: Fish don't have the greatest eyesight, but they detect contrast and movement. And vibration. A lot of lures have little metal beads inside them so they rattle as you reel them in. 'Ch-ch-ch-ch'. The fish feels that vibration in their lateral line before they ever see the bait. 

PO: It really is a whole science. I'm starting to understand why fishing sections in sporting goods stores are so massive. 

Client: Oh, they design lures to catch fishermen, not fish. That's the old saying. They paint them with holographic patterns and realistic 3D eyes. The fish doesn't care about a 3D eye. But the guy with his wallet out at the bait shop sure does. 

PO: [Laughs] The packaging definitely works on me. I'd buy the shiniest one.

Client: We all do. I've got boxes full of lures I've never even tied on a line. Just look pretty sitting in the trays. But my favorite lure of all time? A simple white spinnerbait with a silver blade. Caught more fish on that one cheap lure than anything else combined. 

PO: Sometimes simple is best. Just like the gardening. Just good dirt and water.

Client: Simple is always best. Life gets complicated when you try to force things. You try to force a tomato to grow in bad dirt, it's gonna struggle. You try to catch a fish with a hundred-dollar gadget when it just wants a simple worm, you're gonna go home empty-handed. 

PO: You have to work with nature, not against it.

Client: Kills the mosquitoes, for one. And it hardens off the ground. Walking through the woods on a frosty morning is entirely different. The ground crunches under your boots. The air is so sharp it almost hurts your lungs to breathe in deep. 

PO: I love seeing my breath in the air on the first cold morning.

PO: I've always thought so too. Bright red against pure white. 

Client: We put out black oil sunflower seeds on the porch rail. The cardinals, the chickadees, the little tufted titmice... they'd all fly down, grab a seed, and dart back into the evergreen bushes to crack 'em open. Kept me entertained for hours. 

PO: It sounds like a perfect winter day. 

Client: It was. Makes you appreciate the change of seasons. If it was summer all year round, you wouldn't appreciate the green leaves. If it was winter all year, you wouldn't appreciate the snow. The turning of the wheel is what makes it all work.

Client: You do that. And keep your eye on the squirrels, too. If they start building their nests higher up in the oak trees, that means deep snow is coming. If they build 'em low, it's gonna be a mild winter. 

PO: Are you serious? The squirrels know how deep the snow will be?

Client: Animals know, man. They've been surviving out there a lot longer than we have. We lost our instincts, but they still have theirs. Just gotta pay attention to what they're doing.
