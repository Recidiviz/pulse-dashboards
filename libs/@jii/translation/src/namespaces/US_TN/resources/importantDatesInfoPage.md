## Release Eligibility Date

Your Release Eligibility Date (RED) is the first day you can become eligible to see the Parole Board. Being “eligible” means the Board _may_ choose to grant you parole, but it does not guarantee release. Around your RED, you will be scheduled for a **Parole Hearing Date** — this is the actual date when you go in front of the Board. From that point on, the hearing date is what matters for your release, not the RED. If the Board does not grant parole at your hearing, they may set another hearing date in the future — this is called the **Future Action Date**.

## What does the percent (%) served mean?

Every parole‑eligible sentence has a percent. This is the amount of your sentence you must serve before you are first eligible for parole. Example: A 10‑year sentence at 30% means you would reach RED after 3 years. If it were a 60% sentence, you would reach RED after 6 years.

## Can my Release Eligibility Date move sooner?

Sentence Credits can move your RED earlier. For some people, credits can reduce the time to RED by **up to 30%**. If the crime you are serving time for happened **before January 1, 1988**, credits can reduce that time by **up to 35%**. If your sentence has a mandatory minimum (for example, 85% or 100% sentence), other rules may limit how much sooner the RED can move.

## Expiration Date

The Expiration Date (EXP) is when your sentence ends. You will be released on this date if you are not already on Parole. Sentence Credits may or may not move this date – it depends on your case:

- If your offense was **before July 1, 2024**: Sentence Credits may reduce EXP, depending on your charges.
- If your offense was **on/after July 1, 2024** and your sentence is **under 2 years**: Sentence Credits can reduce EXP.
- If your offense was on/after **July 1, 2024** and your sentence is **2 years or more**: Sentence Credits do **not** change EXP.

If the Judge granted you Jail Credits for time spent in County Jail before sentencing you get credit for the time served. If you got pre-trial behavior credit, those also reduce your EXP. Without Sentence Credits, your Expiration Date would be the same as your Full Expiration.

## Full Expiration

Full Expiration (FXP) is the very last possible day you could be held in prison on the sentence. FXP does **not** change with credits. It is what your Expiration Date would be without Sentence Credits.

For example, if a person started a 10-year sentence on January 1, 2020, their FXP would be January 1, 2030.

## The app says I don’t have an EXP or RED – why not?

This could be for one of a few reasons:

- **Recent arrival at TDOC**: If you recently arrived at TDOC, Sentence Management may still be entering and calculating your sentence dates. This typically happens within 60 days after TDOC receives your sentencing paperwork from the County, but delays in the County sending that paperwork can extend the time.
- **Life sentence**: If you are serving a life sentence without the possibility of parole, you will not see either date. Note that it can still be valuable to track sentence credits if your sentence is commuted to a term of years.
- **Issue processing one of your sentences**: Ask your Counselor to check if this is the case for you. If you have multiple sentences, even one having an issue will prevent your release dates from calculating. This can happen if the County Court that sentenced you made a mistake on your sentencing paperwork. Your Counselor will be able to reach out to Records, or you can write to your County Court.

## Where can I go if I have questions?
This page answers some frequently asked questions about sentence dates. If you have more questions, ask your Counselor. If your Counselor can't answer your question, they will reach out to Records at your facility. From there, Records may be able to answer the question, or Records will reach out to the Sentence Management group at Central Office.
